The Accounting Cycle Overview

The accounting cycle is a holistic, step-by-step process of recording, classifying, and summarizing the economic transactions of a business to ultimately produce useful financial statements. It ensures accuracy and compliance over a specific reporting period (typically a month, quarter, or year). In this module, we will follow Vertex Consulting through its entire first month of operations to demonstrate the cycle perfectly.

  • To master the sequential mechanics of accounting from source documents to post-closing.
  • To understand the dual effect of business transactions (Debits and Credits).
  • To accurately measure net income through adjusting entries (Accruals and Deferrals).
  • To prepare the books for the next operating period via closing and reversing entries.

Step 1: Analysis of Transactions

Definition & Source Documents

The first and most critical step is analyzing economic events to determine their impact on the accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Not all business events are recorded in the books—only those that can be quantified in monetary terms. We identify these transactions through source documents such as official receipts, sales invoices, deposit slips, and purchase orders.

Rules of Debit and Credit

Accounting uses a double-entry system where every transaction affects at least two accounts, keeping the equation in constant balance. "Debit" (Dr) simply means left, and "Credit" (Cr) means right. The normal balance of an account indicates which side increases its value.

Step 2: Journalizing

Once analyzed, transactions are recorded chronologically in the General Journal, often called the "Book of Original Entry." A complete journal entry includes the date, the accounts to be debited (written first and aligned left), the accounts to be credited (written next and indented), the amounts separated into two distinct columns (Debit and Credit), and a brief explanation.

Alternative Methods for Prepayments and Precollections

When paying or receiving cash in advance, an accountant can choose between two methods. Both are correct, but they require different adjustments at month-end.

Step 3: Posting to the Ledger

While the journal records events chronologically, the General Ledger groups transactions by specific account (e.g., all Cash transactions together). This is the "Book of Final Entry." Posting is the mechanical process of transferring debits and credits from the journal to the ledger to determine the updated balance of each account.

Illustration of Posting

Step 4: Unadjusted Trial Balance

After all postings for the period are complete, an unadjusted trial balance is prepared. This is a simple list of all open ledger accounts and their ending balances, arranged in standard chart of accounts order: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenues, and Expenses (ALORE).

Its primary purpose is to mathematically prove that total Debits equal total Credits (the fundamental rule of double-entry accounting). However, a balanced trial balance does not guarantee that no errors were made. For example, if an entire transaction was completely omitted, or posted to the wrong account but on the correct side, the trial balance will still balance.

Step 5a: Adjusting Entries

Under the accrual basis of accounting, revenues are recognized when earned, and expenses are recognized when incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands. At the end of the period, some ledger balances are not perfectly up-to-date. Adjusting entries correct these balances before financial statements are prepared.

Types of Adjustments

  • 1. Deferrals (Prepayments): Cash is paid/received before the expense is incurred or revenue is earned. Examples include Prepaid Rent (adjusting using the asset method creates Rent Expense) and Unearned Revenue.
  • 2. Accruals: Cash will be paid/received after the expense is incurred or revenue is earned. Examples include Accrued Salaries and Accrued Interest Revenue.
  • 3. Estimates: Non-cash allocations like Depreciation and Bad Debts.

Golden Rule: An adjusting entry will NEVER involve the Cash account. It always affects one Balance Sheet account and one Income Statement account.

Step 5b: The Worksheet

The worksheet is an optional, informal working tool used by accountants to organize data for financial statements. It visually connects the unadjusted trial balance, the adjustments, and the final statement columns.

Step-by-step Guide to the Worksheet:

  1. Unadjusted Trial Balance: Copy the initial balances from the ledger.
  2. Adjustments: Enter the adjusting debits and credits. Create new rows for new accounts (like Depreciation Expense) if they aren't listed yet.
  3. Adjusted Trial Balance: Combine the unadjusted balances with the adjustments. (e.g., A debit balance + a debit adjustment = larger debit).
  4. Income Statement: Extend only the Revenue and Expense accounts to these columns.
  5. Balance Sheet: Extend Assets, Liabilities, and Equity (including Drawings) to these columns.
  6. Net Income Balancing: The difference between IS Debits and IS Credits is Net Income (if Credits > Debits). Place this amount in the IS Debit column and the BS Credit column to balance both sets of columns.

Step 6: Preparing Financial Statements

Using the Adjusted Trial Balance (or the worksheet columns), we formally present the financial position and performance to stakeholders. They must be prepared in a strict order because numbers flow from one statement to the next:

  1. Income Statement: Revenues minus Expenses equals Net Income (or Net Loss).
  2. Statement of Changes in Equity: Beginning Capital + Net Income - Withdrawals = Ending Capital.
  3. Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet): Assets = Liabilities + Ending Capital.
  4. Statement of Cash Flows: Identifies cash inflows and outflows (Operating, Investing, Financing).

Step 7: Journalizing and Posting Adjusting Entries

The worksheet created in Step 5 is just an informal tool. The adjustments we discovered and calculated there must now be officially recorded in the accounting system.

We journalize the adjustments in the General Journal and then post them to the General Ledger. This process permanently updates the actual ledger accounts to reflect the accurate, accrual-basis balances shown in the financial statements.

Step 8: Journalizing and Posting Closing Entries

At the end of the year, we must reset all temporary (nominal) accounts to zero so they can start fresh for the next year. Temporary accounts include Revenues, Expenses, and Withdrawals. We do not close permanent (real) accounts like Assets, Liabilities, or Capital.

We use a temporary clearing account called Income Summary. The closing process follows the R-E-I-D sequence:
1. Revenue: Debit revenues to close, Credit Income Summary.
2. Expense: Debit Income Summary, Credit expenses to close.
3. Income Summary: Close the net income (or loss) to Capital.
4. Drawings: Credit withdrawals to close, Debit Capital.

Step 9: Preparing the Post-Closing Trial Balance

This is the third and final trial balance prepared during the cycle. Because we just closed all revenues, expenses, and drawings, this trial balance will only contain permanent (real) accounts: Assets, Liabilities, and ending Capital.

Its sole purpose is to prove the equality of debits and credits of the permanent accounts that will be carried forward into the next accounting year. If this balances, the books are successfully closed for the year.

Step 10: Reversing Entries

Reversing entries are an optional accounting procedure performed on the very first day of the new accounting period. They are the exact opposite of certain adjusting entries made on the last day of the previous period.

They are primarily used for accruals (Accrued Revenues and Accrued Expenses). Reversing an accrual simplifies the routine journalizing of the subsequent cash receipt or payment in the new year, allowing the bookkeeper to record the cash entry normally without worrying about splitting the amount between the prior period liability/receivable and the current period expense/revenue.
