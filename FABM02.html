Nature of Merchandising Operations

Unlike a service business that sells expertise, time, or labor, a merchandising business (like a grocery store or a clothing retailer) buys finished goods and resells them to customers at a markup. This fundamental difference introduces a new, highly significant current asset called Inventory, and a new, major expense category called Cost of Goods Sold (COGS).

The presence of inventory profoundly alters the structure of the income statement. Rather than simply subtracting expenses from revenues to find net income, a merchandiser first calculates Gross Profit by subtracting COGS from Net Sales. This Gross Profit figure must be sufficient to cover all operating expenses and still leave a net profit for the owners.

The operating cycle of a merchandiser is typically longer and more complex than a service firm: Cash is used to buy Inventory, Inventory is sold on account creating Accounts Receivable, and finally, Accounts Receivable is collected to generate Cash. Managing this cash-to-cash cycle efficiently is the primary key to profitability and liquidity.

Documents & Internal Control Procedures

Because physical inventory is easily stolen or damaged, and cash transactions are highly frequent, strict internal controls and proper documentation are practically and legally required to safeguard company assets. A strong internal control system prevents fraud, ensures accurate accounting records, and promotes operational efficiency.

Purchases and Payments Cycle

To prevent unauthorized purchases and ensure the company only pays for goods it actually received and ordered, a robust procurement system is vital.

  • Purchase Requisition: An internal formal request from a department needing goods to the centralized purchasing department. This prevents duplicate orders from different departments.
  • Purchase Order (PO): A legally binding document sent to the external vendor specifying exact quantities, prices, and shipping terms. It authorizes the vendor to ship the goods.
  • Receiving Report: Created by the receiving department when goods arrive. They physically count and inspect the goods without looking at the PO quantities (a control called a "blind check") to ensure accurate counting.
  • Vendor Invoice: The official bill from the supplier demanding payment, detailing what was shipped and the amount owed.
  • Internal Control (The 3-Way Match): Before paying any Accounts Payable, the accounting department matches the Purchase Order, Receiving Report, and Vendor Invoice. If all three match perfectly, a Voucher is prepared authorizing the cash disbursement. This guarantees the company only pays for what it ordered and received.

Sales, Collections, and Memos

On the revenue side, controls must ensure that all shipments are billed, collections are deposited, and returns are verified.

  • Sales Invoice: The primary document supporting a credit sale. It acts as the source document to record Sales and Accounts Receivable.
  • Official Receipt (OR): Issued immediately when cash is collected from cash sales or from collecting Accounts Receivable, providing proof of payment.
  • Debit Memo: A commercial document issued by a buyer to a seller as a means of formally requesting a price reduction. It indicates that the buyer is debiting (decreasing) their Accounts Payable liability to the seller. This is typically used when the buyer returns damaged goods or receives a short shipment.
  • Credit Memo: A commercial document issued by a seller to a buyer acknowledging a reduction in the amount owed. It indicates that the seller is crediting (decreasing) the buyer's Accounts Receivable balance. This is the seller's response to a buyer's Debit Memo, officially granting the Sales Return or Allowance.
  • Internal Control (Separation of Duties): The person who physically handles cash collections must never be the same person who records entries in the Accounts Receivable ledger. This prevents "lapping" (stealing from one customer's payment and covering it with the next). Furthermore, all cash receipts should be deposited intact daily into the bank, and all major payments should be made via check or traceable bank transfer.

Periodic vs. Perpetual Inventory Systems

Merchandisers must choose how they track their physical inventory levels and calculate their cost of goods sold. The choice of system fundamentally changes the journal entries used during the accounting cycle.

Periodic System

Inventory balance is NOT updated continuously. When goods are bought, they are debited to a temporary account called Purchases. When goods are sold, only the revenue is recorded; the reduction in inventory is ignored.

At the end of the accounting cycle, a physical count is mandatory to determine Ending Inventory. COGS is calculated mathematically:
Beg. Inv + Net Purchases - End. Inv = COGS

Perpetual System

Inventory is updated in real-time. Purchases are debited directly to the Inventory asset account.

Every time a sale is made, two entries are required simultaneously: one to record the Sale at retail price, and a second to directly transfer the exact cost from Inventory to COGS. This requires computerized barcode scanners and sophisticated point-of-sale systems.

Terms & FOB Shipping

Credit Terms & Cash Discounts

Terms like 2/10, n/30 mean the buyer gets a 2% discount if they pay within 10 days; otherwise, the net (full) amount is due in 30 days. For the buyer, this is a Purchase Discount (reduces cost of purchases). For the seller, this is a Sales Discount (contra-revenue account). Note: Cash discounts encourage early payment, whereas Trade discounts are simply volume reductions from the catalog price and are never recorded in the accounts.

Shipping Terms (Who pays freight?)

  • FOB Shipping Point (Free On Board at the seller's dock)

    Title transfers when goods leave the seller. The Buyer owns the goods in transit and therefore must pay the freight. The buyer records this cost as Freight In (which is added to the total cost of purchases).

  • FOB Destination (Free On Board at the buyer's door)

    Title transfers only when goods reach the buyer. The Seller owns the goods in transit and must pay the freight. The seller records this cost as Freight Out (which is reported as a normal operating/selling expense).

Step 5: Adjusting Inventory (Two Methods)

In a periodic inventory system, the Inventory account sitting in the unadjusted trial balance still reflects the beginning inventory from the start of the year. It must be updated to the ending physical count. There are two acceptable accounting methods to accomplish this update within the 10-step cycle.

Method 1: The Adjusting Entry Method

We update inventory directly in Step 5 (Adjusting Entries) using the Income Summary account.

  • Entry 1 (Remove Beg. Inv): Debit Income Summary, Credit Inventory (Beg amount).
  • Entry 2 (Record End. Inv): Debit Inventory (End amount), Credit Income Summary.
  • Worksheet Impact: These adjustments go into the "Adjustments" column. Inventory flows to the Balance Sheet. The two Income Summary amounts flow straight to the Income Statement columns (do not net them on the worksheet).

Method 2: The Closing Entry Method

We DO NOT adjust inventory in Step 5. We handle it completely in Step 8 (Closing Entries) while closing out Purchases and COGS accounts.

  • During Closing (Step 8): We Debit Ending Inventory (setting it up), Credit Beginning Inventory (removing it), Credit all Purchase costs (Purchases, Freight In), Debit contra-purchases, and plug the exact difference to Income Summary.
  • Worksheet Impact: Inventory has NO adjustments in Step 5. Beginning Inventory goes straight to the Income Statement Debit column. Ending Inventory is physically written at the bottom of the worksheet and placed in the IS Credit column AND BS Debit column simultaneously.
